The Psychology of Colour

Colour profoundly shapes how we perceive the world around us. Blue tones have long been associated with calm, depth, and reliability — no coincidence that so many banks and technology companies reach for it. Researchers have found that exposure to blue environments can lower heart rate and promote feelings of trust, making it one of the most effective colours in design.

Drawn to Green

Humans evolved surrounded by green. Our eyes have more receptors sensitive to green wavelengths than any other colour, making it the easiest for the eye to process. Studies in environmental psychology consistently show that exposure to green spaces reduces cortisol levels and improves cognitive performance — a response so deeply wired it activates even from looking at images of nature.

The Urgency of Red

Red is the colour of fire, blood, and ripe fruit — signals that demanded immediate attention throughout human evolution. It elevates heart rate, stimulates appetite, and commands focus faster than any other hue. Used carefully, it conveys passion and energy; used carelessly, it overwhelms. The challenge with red in design is knowing precisely when to deploy it.

Warmth and Grounding

Earth tones carry a deep sense of stability and permanence. The warm spectrum of browns and tawny ambers connects us to soil, wood, and stone — the materials humans have built with for millennia. Where cooler colours evoke the sky and sea, these tones anchor. They have seen a quiet renaissance in modern design, valued for their ability to create spaces that feel both contemporary and enduringly human.

Between Blue and Violet

Mauve occupies the ambiguous edge between blue and violet, a colour historically so difficult to produce it was reserved for royalty. When William Perkin accidentally synthesised the first synthetic dye in 1856, he created mauve — and inadvertently launched the modern chemical industry. Today, these muted violet tones appear in design when the goal is sophistication without severity: present enough to make a statement, restrained enough to hold space.
